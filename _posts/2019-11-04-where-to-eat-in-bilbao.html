Bilbao was the culinary highlight of our road trip through the Spanish provinces of Cantabria and the Basque Country. The Basque capital should be on every foodie's bucket list; it's often said that the best Spanish food is served in Bilbao. Intrigued, I took it upon myself to explore the fine art of pintxo cuisine and the Guggenheim Museum. Naturally, it's made me more cultured than two-month-old yoghurt. Here are my tips on where to eat in Bilbao.


Jump to: Exploring Bilbao's Food Scene, or Getting to Bilbao. This post is part of a series about northern Spain. See my other posts about Cantabria and the Basque Country.

Introducing Bilbao

Bilbao is the capital of the Basque Country, an autonomous region in northern Spain. Historically, Bilbao was an industrial port city, but the city has been given a complete makeover over the last decades and is now held in high regard in Spain as the city where things 'work'. Internationally, Bilbao has made a name for itself as a hotspot for high-end cuisine and fine art venues.

Exploring Bilbao's Food Scene

The food was relatively more expensive than what we're used to in Málaga, but the generale rule about dining out in Spain holds true here as well: breaking the piggybank is absolutely unnecessary. I also have to admit that the food in Bilbao, although pricier, is good value; dishes and bites are of a higher quality and more interesting in terms of technique and flavor complexity.

Gure Toki

We kicked off our evening in Bilbao at Gure Toki, an award-winning pintxo (Basque tapas) bar on the photogenic Plaza Nueva. The pintxos were hard to resist, having been put on display on the bar like mouthwatering miniature works of haute cuisine. We carefully and painstakingly weighed our options and had two rounds of pintxos that we washed down with a local txakoli (Basque white wine).

La Viña del Ensanche

We seriously considered ordering more pintxos, but in the end thought it best to go for a little walk and then head to a restaurant for dinner. After a nice walk through the old city centre we went to La Viña del Ensanche. This restaurant specializes in two things: red wine and jamón. And I can only say they do it outstandingly well. We ordered josellinis au gratin: Iberian pork loin with melted cheese and foie on toasted, honeycombed crystal bread, carrilleras (Iberian pork cheeks stewed in red wine) with mashed potato and a bottle of Luís Cañas Rioja to go with it. We knew La Viña served good food, but this exceeded our expectations by lightyears. The combination of the Iberian ham with the foie was exquisite and the pork cheeks were so tender we were given dessert spoons to eat them. As the food was so good, we decided to crown our dinner with desserts: a red fruit gratin and apple tiramisu, both served with cream cheese ice cream. The verdict: suffice it to say that there is footage of me cleaning the plate with my finger.

Restaurante Aralar

After a good night's sleep thanks to the Rioja, we went to the Guggenheim Museum the next morning. Having spent the entire morning walking around the museum, our stomachs were ready for our final feast in Bilbao before heading to the airport. We decided to go to a common lunchroom. In Spain, workers often have long lunch hours and go to a restaurant that has a set (three-course) menu that is often very reasonably priced. We found a little gem called Aralar right before the lunch rush hour. We ordered a warm chard salad with ham (because I was eager to have some veggies) and cod in puff pastry as starters. As mains we had bonito fish with caramelized onions, and pork loin in a reduction of Pedro Ximenez (sherry wine). For dessert, we had gin & tonic cheesecake and carrot cake. That doesn't sound too bad, huh? It was delicious, and the bill almost gave it an even sweeter aftertaste. Including the bottle of Rioja, it cost a mere 17 euros per person. Bellies full and satisfied, we were sad to leave Bilbao as we felt there probably were so many more foodie gems. I can only hope that we'll get the chance to help ourselves to a second serving of Bilbao.

Getting to Bilbao

Have I wet your appetite for a trip to Bilbao? The city has an international airport (European flights). Getting to the city centre from the airport is easy with the airport shuttle service; buses run every 30 mins. Alternatively you can take the bus or train from major cities in Spain. Flixbus services major cities throughout Europe.
