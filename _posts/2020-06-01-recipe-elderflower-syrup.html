During my daily walks, I have started to notice that a particular spring fragrance has filled the air: that of elderflower blossom. I love the smell of elderflower so much I could drown in it. As it turns out, you could, in theory, because these blossoms can be made into a delicious elderflower syrup, or elderflower cordial, which is great for making lemonade, or sprucing up cocktails (especially G&T) and desserts.


Jump to: Recipe.

Foraging Elderflower

Elderflowers grow in Europe, northwest Africa and southwest Asia. In late May and June it's hard to miss the distinct fragrance of elderflower blossoms when out in nature, because these bushes are not that hard to find! They grow in forests, on the roadside, preferably in sunny spots. When you go out looking for elderflower, look for a a bush with a rough, rugged brown trunk. The leaves of the elderflower are oval and smooth. The flowers grow in corymbs, with many stems leading the flowers to the same flat level. These fans of flowers can be 10-25 cm in diameter, whereas the flowers are only half a centimeter big and have five petals.

Recipe: Elderflower cordial

Ingredients (for 1 liter)

  • 15 elderflower corymbs
    •
  • 1 liter of water
    •
  • 500 g of sugar
    •
  • 1 lemon, sliced
    •

Method

  1. Remove the green stems from the flowers (stems make the syrup bitter). Also check for bugs!
  2. Put the flowers and lemon slices in a pot.
  3. Bring the water to a boil and pour over the flowers and lemon.
  4. Cover and leave to infuse for 24 hours.
  5. The next day, add the sugar to the water and flower mixture and bring to a boil.
  6. Thoroughly clean (with hot water or in the oven) a glass container for the syrup.
  7. Once all the sugar has dissolved and the syrup is bubbling, take it off the heat.
  8. Use a sieve (and funnel) to pour the syrup into the container.
