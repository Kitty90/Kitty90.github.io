Although COVID-19 forced us to curtail our travels in Asia, the measures in the Netherlands are relatively lenient, so we still enjoy the possibility to go out and explore my home region and country. Contrary to what many believe, the Netherlands boasts several national parks and conservation areas, De Veluwe being one of the most famous ones. Read on to learn about De Veluwe, hiking routes and our hike through Deelerwoud.

April weather has been so surprisingly lovely since our return from Asia that we have tried to make the most of it by going on hiking and cycling trips in the neighborhood. Last weekend we decided to go a bit farther afield and drove to a nature reserve called 'de Veluwe'.

About De Veluwe

De Veluwe is a nature reserve that consists of two national parks: De Hoge Veluwe National Park and National Park Veluwezoom (Dutch only). Additionally, the region comprises other nature reserves such as Deelerwoud, Leuvenumse Bossen, Planken Wambuis, Wolfheze, and Oud Groevenbeek.

The entire area is located in the heart of the Netherlands, roughly nestled between the cities of Arnhem, Apeldoorn, Amersfoort and Harderwijk. De Hoge Veluwe National Park is also home to the Kröller-Müller Museum which boasts the second-largest Van Gogh collection in the world, as well as works by Monet, Mondriaan, Picasso, and Seurat.

Flora and Fauna in De Veluwe

De Veluwe is known for deciduous, coniferous and mixed forests; (purple) moor- and grasslands; sand drifts; lakes; and abundant wildlife. Nature enthusiasts have a good chance of spotting the Dutch 'big five': red deer, roe deer, wild boars, foxes and mouflons (a wild sheep native to Corse and Sardinia). Additionally, the park is teeming with birds and Scottish Highland cows roam around to their hearts' content.

Our Hike through Deelerwoud

We decided to do a 9 km hike through Deelerwoud, an area between the two national parks. The moor was relatively barren and brown, due to the dry weather we've been having in the Netherlands. We were glad to enjoy the sunshine though, as it imbued the landscape with the excitement of spring. The route takes you around a moor where Scottish Highlanders are grazing. Contrary to what its name suggests, the Deelerwoud route does not lead you through wooded areas; it mostly guides you along a moor. Although this is quite an exceptional landscape in the Netherlands, it does tend to get a rather monotonous walking along it for 9 kilometres. Nonetheless, we had a beautiful day in nature and felt like we were in a different country altogether! We will definitely come back to do another route if the weather and time permit it.


Getting to and Hiking in De Veluwe

Some parts of De Veluwe are free, whereas others are not. Hikers visiting De Hoge Veluwe National Park are required to pay an entry fee, ranging between 5 and 10 euros. Surcharges apply for parking, entry on horseback, or entry to the Kröller-Müller Museum. Visitors can get a map of the park for an additional 2 euros. There are visitor centers at Hoenderloo, Otterloo and Schaarsbergen. The centers can be reached by car, or by train (Arnhem, Apeldoorn, or Ede-Wageningen) and bus. It is best to plan our visit using the Dutch public transport web app 9292.nl.

The remaining regions in de Veluwe are best reached by car. These regions are maintained by Dutch nature preserve organization Natuurmonumenten. Entry to these areas is free of charge; however, Natuurmonumenten has announced that it plans to start charging parking fees to visitors coming by car.
