Saturday usually is my market day. Which means that on Saturday morning, my pantry and fridge should be nearly empty and it's time to perform my consumerist duties and stock up on fresh vegetables for the coming week. On Friday I discovered that I still had zucchinis, aubergines and bell peppers in the fridge and they had to make space. So, I had the perfect excuse to make a ratatouille, but it got me wondering, what is a traditional ratatouille, really?


Jump to recipe.

In the name of culinary research, I delved into the world of French cooking and recipe websites. It has been a few years since I studied French and I was plowing through French articles and posts on ratatouille's origin, trying to unravel the mysteries of both language and cuisine, but the findings were inconclusive. To my linguistic heart's content, I discovered that the Dutch word 'ratjetoe', a word referring to a little mess, is derived from French Ratatouille. The name couldn't have been more fitting, as I found myself in a ratjetoe of theories about the dish's origin.

It turns out, there is no real Ratatouille, although there seems to be a traditional method of preparing the dish. Regarding its origin, the general consensus is that Ratatouille is Provençale (France), but the Catalans (Spain) claim the dish, too, albeit under the name of Samfaina. All these varieties from different regions seemed to have one thing in common: there are rules to preparing ratatouille. The most important and universal rule I've found is that the vegetables should all be cooked separately before being added to the tomato sauce, to ensure that they maintain their own flavors. So, there was my first step in the right direction.

However, I found out that, apart from cooking the vegetables  separately, all bets are off! Some use only thyme to flavor the dish, others use a plethora of Provencal and Mediterranean herbs like rosemary, parsley, basil and laurel. Some prepare the dish in the oven, others in a pot, some cut the vegetables in cubes, others imitate the popularized Disney Ratatouille with vegetable slices. My head was spinning, Mon dieu! I didn't know what's wrong and what's right anymore. Did it really matter, if the taste was right anyway? I didn't think so. This is my little mess, my 'ratjetoe'; an authentic ratatouille according to some, and let that be the end of it.

Recipe: Ratatouille

Ingredients (serves 4)

  • 2 zucchinis

  • 2 aubergines
    •
  • 1 yellow bell pepper

  • 1 green bell pepper

  • 5 small tomatoes

  • 1 onion
    •
  • 4 cloves of garlic
    •
  • bouquet garni (rosemary, thyme, parsley)
    •
  • salt and pepper
    •
  • 1 tbsp sugar
    •
  • olive oil
    •

Method

  1. Cut the aubergines into cubes, sprinkle with a generous amount of salt and put them in a colander over a pot.
  2. Cut the zucchinis into cubes as well, sprinkle with salt and leave to rest in a bowl.
  3. Cut the bell peppers into chunks.
  4. To make the tomato sauce, put 500 ml of water in a pot and bring to a boil.
  5. Cut shallow crosses in the bottom of the tomatoes and add them to the pot when the water is boiling.
  6. After 30 seconds, take out the tomatoes and peel them, starting at the edges of the crosses on the bottom of the tomatoes.
  7. While the tomatoes are cooling down, finely cut the onion and 1 clove of garlic.
  8. Put a lug of olive oil in a large pot and add the onion and garlic, fry on low heat.
  9. Cut the tomatoes into quarters, de-seed them and cut them into dices.
  10. Add the tomatoes and the bouquet garni to the onion and the garlic. Add half a cup of water and a tbsp of sugar as well. Cover the pot and leave to simmer for 20 minutes.
  11. Rinse the salt off the aubergines and put a frying pan on medium heat.
  12. Add a lug of olive oil, one clove of garlic and a pinch of thyme to the pan and add the aubergine after 1 minute.
  13. Fry the aubergine for about 15 minutes, until cooked through, take it out of the pan and set aside.
  14. In the same pan, fry the zucchini cubes with a clove of garlic, a pinch of thyme and some olive oil for about 10 minutes.
  15. Check if the tomato sauce is not becoming too dry and add a bit of water if necessary.
  16. Take the zucchini out of the frying pan and set aside.
  17. In the same pan, now cook the peppers for 10 minutes with a lug of olive oil, one clove of garlic and a pinch of thyme.
  18. When the bell peppers are cooked, take the bouquet garni out of the tomato sauce.
  19. Add the peppers, the aubergine and the zucchini to the tomato sauce, mix thoroughly, season with salt and pepper and leave to simmer for another 25 minutes.
  20. After 25 minutes, serve the ratatouille with some rustic bread.
